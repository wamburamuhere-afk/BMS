Phase One
Security Review & Clean-Up
Risk Identified & Removed

What we found

As part of a routine security review of the system, we checked every single page and tool sitting on the live server against the list of pages the system is actually supposed to expose. Out of just under 300 items found at the top level of the website, only 15 turned out to be legitimate, currently-used parts of the system.

The remaining 284 were leftovers from the system's early development — one-off scripts that had been used years ago to check or repair data during setup, and were never removed. Because of how they were placed, anyone who found or guessed the right web address could open them directly, without logging in at all. A number of these old tools were capable of directly changing information in the database.

Why this matters

A tool that can alter company data and doesn't require a login is a serious weak point — even if it was never actually misused, its mere presence is a risk that has no place on a live business system handling financial and operational data.

What we did about it

Every one of the 284 old tools was moved out of the publicly reachable part of the system into a locked-away area that cannot be accessed from a web browser at all. The normal system — everything staff and customers actually use — was not touched and continues to work exactly as before. We also added a permanent, automated check that will immediately flag it if anything like this is ever accidentally exposed again in the future.

Before
284 unprotected internal tools reachable from the open internet, some able to alter company data.
After
All 284 locked away and unreachable; a standing check now guards against this recurring.
Status

This fix has been fully built and tested. It is one internal release step away from being active on the live production system — expected to be finalised in the next session.

Phase Two
Making Money-Handling Fail Safely
Strengthened

The underlying problem

Many everyday actions in the system — paying an employee, raising an invoice, removing a warehouse, updating a customer record — actually involve several related updates happening one after another behind the scenes. Normally that happens instantly and without issue. But if something interrupts the process partway through — a lost connection, a server restart, a timing clash between two people acting at once — some systems can be left in a "half-done" state: for example, an employee marked as paid without the payment actually being recorded in the books, or a stock removal that deletes some records but not others.

We reviewed every process in the system that performs this kind of multi-step update and found several — across payroll, invoicing, warehouse management, and customer records — that were not fully protected against this kind of interruption.

Why this matters

In a system that handles company money and stock, a "half-done" update isn't a cosmetic glitch — it can mean a payment that looks made but isn't recorded, or an invoice accidentally duplicated on a retry. These are the kinds of errors that are expensive and time-consuming to trace and correct after the fact.

What we did about it

Every one of these processes has been rebuilt so that each multi-step update is now "all-or-nothing": either every part of it completes successfully, or the whole thing is automatically cancelled and the records are left exactly as they were before the attempt. There is no longer a scenario where an interruption leaves a partial, inconsistent result. This protection now covers payroll payments, invoicing, warehouse stock removal, and the main customer-record update processes.

Payroll
Each employee's payment now completes fully or not at all — no more partially-paid batches.
Invoicing
Removed a scenario where a retry after a failure could create a duplicate invoice.
Warehouse Records
Stock removal no longer risks deleting some records while leaving others behind.
Customer & Sales Records
Update processes across sales and customer management given the same protection.

An extra safeguard: automatic accuracy checks

On top of the fixes above, the system now automatically verifies — every time a change happens — that all four core financial reports (Trial Balance, Income Statement, Balance Sheet, and Cash Flow) still add up correctly and agree with one another. Any inconsistency would now be caught immediately by the system itself, rather than being discovered later during a manual review.

Phase Three
Customer Order Workflow — New Feature
Launched

Where we started

Customer purchase orders (the formal orders customers place with us) previously lived as a simple note attached to a customer's profile. There was no structured review step, no formal approval, and no direct connection to the delivery or invoicing that followed — everything downstream had to be handled and cross-checked manually.

What was built

A complete, standalone Customer Order module, built to the same standard as our existing Purchase Order process. A customer order now goes through a proper three-step approval — prepared, reviewed, and approved by the appropriate level of authority — before it can move forward, exactly mirroring how we already control outgoing purchase orders.

From there, the order flows directly into a delivery record and then into an invoice, without anyone needing to re-type quantities, prices, or customer details. Every document in that chain carries a visible reference back to where it started, so at any point it's possible to trace an invoice back through its delivery to the original customer order that triggered it.

We also reorganised the Sales menu so that the steps — order, order confirmation, delivery, invoice — appear in the natural sequence they happen in, rather than scattered across unrelated sections.

Why this matters

This closes a control gap: large or high-value customer orders now go through the same level of formal sign-off as our purchasing does, and the whole order-to-invoice trail is recorded in one connected system instead of living across separate notes and manual cross-referencing.

Before
A note on the customer's file; no formal approval; no link to delivery or invoicing.
After
A full order → approval → delivery → invoice chain, fully traceable, properly authorised.

Phase Four
Payroll Processing Fix
Urgent Fix

A recent enhancement to how employer statutory contributions are calculated had introduced an oversight that caused the "Process Payroll" function to fail outright for every single employee, every time it was run. Nobody could be paid through the system until this was resolved.

We traced the fault to a technical gap left behind by that earlier change, corrected it, and confirmed that payroll now processes normally end-to-end for every employee. We also checked for any leftover partial records from the earlier failed attempts and confirmed none were created — no cleanup was required.

Phase Five
Sales Dashboard & Customer Data Fixes
Fixed

Dashboard issues

The dashboard the sales team uses to track leads, follow-ups, and top performers was displaying blank charts and tables that appeared permanently stuck loading, for some staff. This has been corrected — all charts and summary tables on the dashboard now display correctly.

Bulk customer/lead upload

The tool that lets staff upload a list of customers or sales leads in bulk from a spreadsheet had become unreachable, and once restored, had a few rough edges — the file selection did not always respond, and the upload button could get stuck after certain errors. All of this has been corrected and the tool has been tested with a range of file conditions.

Phase Six
Smaller Corrections & Consistency Fixes
Fixed
Warehouse Selection
A few screens allowed a warehouse to be picked that didn't belong to the project being worked on. Now consistent everywhere, reducing the risk of stock being recorded against the wrong location.
Document Numbering
Changing the company's document numbering format now correctly updates existing records when they are next edited, instead of silently staying on the old format.
Delivery Note Screens
Delivery note options now only appear on the correct screen for the correct department (Purchases vs. Sales), removing a source of confusion for staff.
Order Entry Accuracy
The unit of measure on a product (kilograms, litres, pieces, etc.) can no longer be accidentally changed after the product is selected on an order or invoice.
Release Process
An outdated internal safety check was blocking new updates from reaching the live system after the Customer Order feature was added. Corrected so releases now go live smoothly.
Housekeeping
Confirmed a handful of previously completed database updates were properly and permanently recorded, closing a small gap that could otherwise have caused inconsistency between environments.
