1. The one big idea (read this first)

Every shilling that moves is written into one shared book — the General Ledger — using double-entry. All four reports are just different views of that same one book. Nothing is typed into a report by hand.

In accounting there is a golden rule: nothing happens on only one side. If money lands in the bank, it had to come from somewhere (a customer paying, a sale, an owner's deposit). So every event is recorded as at least two lines that are equal and opposite — a Debit and a matching Credit. They must always sum to the same total. That is “double-entry”.

In BMS this shared book is two database tables working together:

journal_entriesOne row per event (the date, description, who did it, which project, and a status).
journal_entry_itemsThe lines of that event — each one is a debit or credit on a specific account, with an amount.

Two rules make the whole system trustworthy:

  • One door in. Every money event is posted through a single helper, postLedgerEntry(). It refuses to save anything unless the debits equal the credits. So the book can never go crooked one event at a time.
  • Only “posted” counts. An entry has a status: draft, posted, void or reversed. Every report reads only posted entries. Drafts and voided items are ignored — they are work-in-progress, not the truth.
Why this matters (a real example). Until recently the Trial Balance accidentally added up some un-posted lines, which made it look ~627 million out of balance. The fix was to count only posted lines. After the fix the books balance to the cent (Debits = Credits = exactly the same number). This is the difference between a report that looks official and one that is actually true.

Each account knows what kind of thing it is, from the account_types table. That single fact decides which report — and which section of it — the account belongs to:

2. Debit & Credit in everyday words

You don't need to be an accountant to read the reports, but two words help:

Debit = value coming IN to an account Cash account goes up → debit it. A cost happens → debit the expense. (Debit-natural accounts: assets, expenses.)
Credit = value going OUT of an account Cash account goes down → credit it. A sale earns income → credit the revenue. (Credit-natural accounts: liabilities, equity, income.)

That's the whole trick. Below, every money event is shown as its Debit/Credit pair so you can see the two sides with your own eyes.

Tanzania reality (built into these doors). VAT is 18% and a fiscalised EFD receipt must be issued when payment is received. 🧪 Try it yourself — prove a sale and a payment are real

  1. Create a sales invoice for a customer: 1 item at 100,000, VAT 18% → grand total 118,000. Approve it.
  2. Open Reports → Income Statement. Sales Revenue should rise by 100,000 (the VAT is not income — it's money you hold for TRA).
  3. Open Reports → Balance Sheet. Accounts Receivable rises by 118,000 and Output VAT Payable (a liability) rises by 18,000.
  4. Now record a payment of 118,000 into your bank account.
  5. Re-open the Balance Sheet: Receivable drops back by 118,000, Bank rises by 118,000. Open the Cash Flow — that 118,000 now shows as cash received from operations.

If every one of those moved exactly as written, the money-in path is sound.

4. Money going OUT — every door

Same principle in reverse. Each outflow posts a balanced entry. Most expenses post in two stages — first the cost is recognised (you owe it), then later it is paid (cash leaves). This is “accrual accounting”, and it is what makes the Income Statement honest about costs even before cash moves.

Tanzania reality (built into these doors). An expense needs a valid EFD receipt to reclaim input VAT. Payroll carries the full statutory set — PAYE (progressive, first 270,000/month tax-free), NSSF 20% (10% employer + 10% employee), SDL 3.5% (employers with ≥10 staff) and WCF 0.5% — each remitted to its authority by the deadline. Supplier payments may carry VAT-withholding (3% goods / 6% services) and 2% government WHT.

🧪 Try it yourself — prove an expense touches the right places

  1. Create an expense, e.g. Fuel 50,000, and approve it (don't pay yet).
  2. Income Statement → Expenses rise by 50,000. Balance Sheet → Accrued Expenses (a liability) rises by 50,000. Cash has not moved yet — correct.
  3. Now mark it paid from the bank.
  4. Balance Sheet → Accrued Expenses drops 50,000, Bank drops 50,000. Cash Flow → shows 50,000 cash out under operating. Income Statement is unchanged (the cost was already counted at step 1). That two-step behaviour is accrual accounting working.

5. The four reports — subsection by subsection

All four read the same one ledger (posted entries only) and group it by the account categories in the table above. They differ only in which accounts they show and how they arrange them. Here is exactly where each subsection's number comes from.

One engine, four views. The calculations live in one file, core/financial_reports.php, with one function per report: glProfitLoss() (Income Statement), glBalanceSheet(), glCashFlow(), and glTrialBalance(). Because they share the same source, they cannot disagree — and a built-in guard, assertLedgerBalanced(), can prove at any moment that Debits = Credits and Assets = Liabilities + Equity.

5a. Income Statement (also called Profit & Loss)

Question it answers: “Over this period, did we make a profit?” It shows income earned and costs incurred between two dates — so it uses the flow in the window, never opening balances.

5b. Balance Sheet (Statement of Financial Position)

Question it answers: “On this date, what do we own, what do we owe, and what's left for the owners?” It shows cumulative balances up to the chosen date (everything since inception).

SubsectionWhere the number comes fromHow it's calculated
Assets
bank, receivables, stock, fixed assets		Every asset accountDebits − Credits, cumulative to the date
Liabilities
suppliers, taxes owed, deposits held		Every liability accountCredits − Debits, cumulative
Equity (paid-in)Every equity accountCredits − Debits, cumulative
Retained Earningsderived from the Income Statement sideAll income − all costs, from inception to the date — folded into equity automatically
Balanced?the integrity testAssets must equal Liabilities + Equity. If not, the page says so plainly.
Why it always balances (when the data is clean). Because every entry has Debits = Credits, it's a mathematical fact that Assets = Liabilities + Equity + (Income − Costs). The Balance Sheet folds “Income − Costs” into equity as Retained Earnings, so the two sides match by construction. If they ever don't, the cause is bad opening data or an unclassified account — and the page surfaces it instead of hiding it.

Live page: Reports → Balance Sheet → reads the posted ledger the same way glBalanceSheet() does.

5c. Cash Flow Statement

Question it answers: “Where did actual cash come from and go, this period?” Profit is not cash (you can be profitable but cash-poor). This statement tracks only the bank & cash accounts.

SubsectionWhere the number comes fromMeaning
Operating activitiesCash entries whose other side is income, an expense, a receivable/payable, stockCash from the day-to-day business
Investing activitiesCash entries whose other side is a fixed asset (codes 1-3xxx / non-current)Buying or selling long-term assets
Financing activitiesCash entries whose other side is equityOwner capital in, drawings out
Net change in cashthe proofOperating + Investing + Financing — must equal the bank balance movement
Opening + change = Closingcash accounts at start and endTies back to the Balance Sheet's cash line
How it's built (the clever bit). The system looks at every entry that touched a cash/bank account, then reads the other side of that entry to decide the category. Because debits always equal credits, the three sections are guaranteed to add back up to the real change in the bank balance. So this statement reconciles by construction — it can't drift away from reality like a hand-built one can.

Live page: Reports → Cash Flow → cash_flow_gl.php → get_cash_flow.php → glCashFlow(). Direct method (IAS 7).

5d. Trial Balance

Question it answers: “Is the whole ledger internally consistent — do all debits equal all credits?” It is the accountant's pre-flight check before trusting the other three. It lists every account with a balance and proves the totals match.

SubsectionWhere the number comes fromHow it's calculated
Each account rowEvery account with any opening balance or posted activityIts net balance, placed in the Debit or Credit column by which side it lands on
Section subtotalsgrouped by categoryAssets, Liabilities, Equity, Revenue, Other Income, COGS, Expenses, Finance Costs
Grand Total Debit / Creditthe proofThe two columns must be equal. If not → a red “does not balance” banner with the difference.
Net Profit (derived)the income & cost sections(Revenue + Other Income) − (COGS + Expenses + Finance Costs) — the same figure the Income Statement shows
The bug that was just fixed (so you can trust this page). The Trial Balance used to accidentally include un-posted (draft) lines in its totals, showing a phantom ≈627,000,000 imbalance. It now counts posted entries only. Verified on the live data, the two columns are now exactly equal (Debit = Credit = 15,474,270,147.68; difference 0.00).

Live page: Reports → Trial Balance → app/constant/reports/trial_balance.php, mirroring glTrialBalance().

🧪 Try it yourself — confirm the books are honest

  1. Open Reports → Trial Balance. The Total Debits and Total Credits cards should show the same number, and “Balance Integrity” should read BALANCED.
  2. Note the Net Profit figure at the foot. Open the Income Statement for the year — the Net Profit should match.
  3. Open the Balance Sheet. Retained Earnings should equal that same all-time Net Profit, and Assets = Liabilities + Equity.

When all three agree, you've proven the four reports are different windows onto one true book.
